### February 14, 2026

As I sit here in my Kathmandu office, pouring over weather forecasts and climate reports for the upcoming climbing season on Everest, I'm struck by a stark realization: the mountain we once knew is no longer the same. The rhythms of the seasons have shifted, the winds are more unpredictable, and the snowpack is thinner than ever before.

I remember my first ascent of Everest like it was yesterday. It was 2007, and the weather was as reliable as an old friend – a spring storm would roll in around noon, bringing with it fierce gusts and a blanket of fresh powder. We'd dig in for the night, knowing that come dawn, the skies would clear, and we'd send (that's mountaineering speak for make a move) up the mountain.

Fast forward to today, and the rules have changed. The beta (climbing lingo for information or advice) I'm getting from seasoned guides is more about anticipating extreme weather events than counting on predictable patterns. And it's not just about the big picture – the daily decisions we make on the crag (or in this case, the high-altitude slopes of Everest) are now influenced by a complex interplay of climate factors.



### Weather Whiplash: The New Normal

The Himalayas have always been prone to rapid changes in weather. A snowstorm could roll in overnight, or a clear blue sky turn into a raging blizzard in minutes. But there's something different about the current trend – an increasing frequency and intensity of extreme weather events.

In 2019, I was part of a team that attempted to climb Everest via the Northeast Ridge route. We encountered conditions unlike anything we'd ever seen before – temperatures soared above freezing during the day, only to drop to -40°C at night. The snowpack was so thin in places that we had to navigate through exposed rock and ice, with nothing but our crampons and axes for protection.



### Climbing in a Changing Climate: Strategies for Success

So how do we adapt? Here are some key takeaways from my research:

* **Stay flexible**: No longer can you plan every detail of your ascent weeks or months in advance. Be prepared to adjust your itinerary based on real-time weather forecasts.

* **Pack accordingly**: Bring gear that can withstand extreme conditions, including windproof jackets and pants, a warm hat, and gloves rated for -50°C or colder.

* **Monitor local conditions**: Talk to guides and other climbers who've been on the mountain recently. They may have valuable insights into current snow conditions, ice formations, and weather patterns.



### Conclusion: Climbing in Unpredictable Times

As I look up at Everest from my office window, I'm reminded of why we climb this mountain – for its sheer beauty, its unforgiving power, and the sense of awe it inspires. But as the climate continues to shift and change, so too must our approach to climbing this magnificent peak.

To navigate unpredictable weather patterns on Everest, you need more than just beta and experience; you need a deep understanding of the mountain's rhythms and a willingness to adapt in the face of uncertainty. That's the new normal on Everest – and it's what makes every ascent, every send, so thrilling and so treacherous.
